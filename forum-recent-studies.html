Creatine plus resistance training still looks like a strong lean-mass play across age groups

A recent meta-analysis on PubMed found that pairing resistance training with creatine supplementation improved fat-free mass in adults under 50 and in adults 50 and over. The useful practical read is that creatine still looks relevant whether the goal is performance support in younger lifters or preserving lean tissue in older adults.

  • The signal was strongest for fat-free mass, not just scale weight.
  • This fits well with periods where training quality and recovery are already consistent.
  • It does not mean creatine replaces programming, sleep, or protein intake.

Nutrition plus exercise continues to outperform nutrition alone for sarcopenia-related outcomes

This review looked at older adults with sarcopenia and found that combining nutritional supplementation with exercise was more useful than supplementation by itself for a range of outcomes tied to strength and function. The big implication is that "supportive nutrition" works best when it is attached to actual training or structured movement.

  • The pattern supports pairing protein-oriented support with real activity, not treating supplements as the whole intervention.
  • This matters most for older adults trying to protect strength, mobility, and independence.
  • It also reinforces the basic Odeology idea that nutrition and training should be programmed together.
Topic: sarcopenia Population: older adults Source: PubMed 41350709

Time-restricted eating with resistance training may help fat-mass reduction, but tradeoffs still matter

A newer review on time-restricted eating paired with resistance training suggests there may be modest fat-mass advantages in some settings, but the evidence is not a blank check for everyone to compress their eating window. Compliance, total protein intake, and training performance still decide whether the setup helps or backfires.

  • TRE seems more useful when it makes calorie control easier, not when it cuts protein feedings too hard.
  • Lifters in a deficit still need to protect training quality and recovery.
  • This is a "maybe useful tool," not a universal upgrade.

Exercise, sedentary time, and adiposity still cluster together the way most coaches would expect

This recent paper reviewed the relationship between physical activity, sedentary behavior, and adiposity patterning. The practical message is not revolutionary, but it is useful: training sessions matter, and the rest of the day still matters too. You cannot fully out-program a routine that is otherwise very inactive.

  • Structured exercise and background movement both shape body-composition outcomes.
  • High sedentary time can weaken the bigger picture even if workouts are in place.
  • This is especially relevant for desk-heavy lifters trying to lean out.
Topic: adiposity and inactivity Population: adults Source: PubMed 40859970

Higher protein concentration may still help weight-loss maintenance when the hard part becomes staying there

The PREVIEW study follow-up on dietary macronutrient composition and protein concentration focused on maintenance rather than just the first drop in body weight. That is useful because a lot of people can lose weight for a stretch, but far fewer can stabilize the result. Higher-protein setups still appear relevant for appetite control and maintenance behavior.

  • The maintenance phase deserves its own strategy, not just a weaker version of the fat-loss phase.
  • Protein concentration may help adherence and satiety after the initial cut.
  • This is the kind of paper that matters for long-term body-composition planning, not just “before and after” pushes.
